# Networking Strategy

This document covers how to build professional visibility and a client pipeline throughout the 18-month program — starting in Month 1, not Month 18.

---

## Why This Starts in Month 1

The mistake most consultants make: treating networking as something you do after you're ready. By Month 18 you have 5 certs and 6 portfolio projects. But your first client won't come from a cert — they'll come from a relationship built 6-12 months earlier. Start now.

---

## The Four Channels

### 1. LinkedIn (Primary Channel)

LinkedIn is the primary B2B platform for consultants targeting enterprise security buyers — CISOs, security architects, and IT directors. This is where PAM buyers are. For anyone transitioning into consulting, it is the highest-leverage platform to invest time in during the entire 18 months.

**What to publish and when — unified content calendar:**

There are two types of LinkedIn content. Keep them separate in your mind:

- **Short posts** (200-400 words, no formatting required): observations, lab findings, quick lessons, questions. Low effort, high frequency.
- **Long-form articles** (800-1,500 words, structured with headers): technical walkthroughs, lessons learned, methodology explanations. High effort, low frequency.

| Period | Short Posts | Long Articles | Total effort/week |
|:------:|:-----------:|:-------------:|:-----------------:|
| M1-8 | 0 (profile building only) | 0 | ~15 min/day profile optimization |
| M9-10 | 1 per week | 0 | ~30 min/week |
| M11-14 | 1-2 per week | 1 per 6-8 weeks | ~45 min/week |
| M15-18 | 2-3 per week | 1 per month | ~60-90 min/week |

This resolves the conflict between documents that said "2-3 times per week" (short posts, correct for M15+) and "one article every 6-8 weeks" (long articles, correct throughout). Both are right — they describe different content types.

**M9**: First post — short lab note: *"What surprised me about CyberArk CPM vs Wallix Password Manager."* Keep it authentic and specific. Authenticity outperforms polish at this stage.

**M11**: First long-form article. Suggested topics (in order of audience interest):
1. "Active Directory attack paths that CyberArk PAM would have blocked" — resonates with both security teams and PAM buyers
2. "Wallix vs CyberArk: what I learned deploying both" — unique, high credibility
3. "Conjur Docker setup: what the documentation doesn't tell you" — DevOps audience
4. "eJPT preparation: honest breakdown of what worked"

**M15 onward**: Post 2-3 times per week. Short posts only at this frequency — a HackTheBox finding, a PAM configuration tip, a question to your network. Save long articles for every 4-6 weeks.

**M18**: Profile complete, 6+ long articles published, 500+ relevant connections.

**Profile optimization:**

- Headline: include CyberArk, PAM, OSCP (once earned), and pentesting.
- About section: write a 3-sentence version of the dual-track value proposition — you can assess AND remediate the privilege-related attack surface, not just one or the other.
- Featured section: pin your 6 portfolio projects as GitHub links. This is the first thing a prospective client should see after your headline.

### 2. CyberArk Community (Highest-ROI Channel for PAM Work)

CyberArk Commons (community.cyberark.com) is where PAM practitioners ask real implementation questions. Answering questions there builds reputation among exactly the people who hire PAM consultants — other implementers, architects, and technical buyers. This channel has the highest return on time for PAM-specific work because the audience is already self-selected.

**What to do:**

- Create your account in M1.
- Read actively M1-6. Learn what problems practitioners actually face in the field. This also informs your study priorities — the questions people ask in the community are more revealing than any exam guide.
- Answer at least 2 questions per month starting M7, even straightforward ones ("how do I configure CredFiles," "what ports does the Vault use," etc.).
- By M12, aim to be a recognizable name in the community. People will begin to associate your username with reliable answers.
- This channel directly generates consulting opportunities. People who see you helping others will reach out. It is not theoretical — it is how many independent PAM consultants found their first engagements.

### 3. Security Conferences

**CyberArk Impact (Annual, usually April-May):**

- The primary CyberArk vendor event. This is where CyberArk partners, implementation consultants, and enterprise customers gather in one place.
- Attend in Year 2 — May 2027, during M13. Registration is typically $500-1,500, or free for CyberArk authorized partners and customers.
- Your goal at Impact is not to sell yourself but to meet people. Target CyberArk authorized partner firms and independent implementation consultants. These are the people who either hire subcontractors or make referrals.
- Bring business cards and a one-page summary of your portfolio projects. Not a sales document — just a conversation artifact.

**BSides Security Conferences (Local, Free or $20-50):**

- BSides events are practitioner-focused, affordable, and held in most major cities throughout the year.
- Attend at least 2 BSides events during the 18 months, starting no earlier than M6 — by then you will have enough experience to have real conversations.
- Submit a talk proposal at M14-15. A 20-minute talk on "How I used BloodHound to find attack paths that CyberArk PAM would have blocked" is a strong submission — it is specific, dual-track, and bridges offense and defense in a way that is relevant to both audiences. Getting accepted to speak at a BSides is a real credential, not just an activity.

**DEF CON and Black Hat (Las Vegas, August):**

- DEF CON runs approximately $360, cash only. Attend during Phase 3 — August 2027, M16 — if your budget allows.
- The PAM and identity security villages are the most relevant areas. Prioritize those over general sessions.
- Black Hat is significantly more expensive ($2,000+). Skip it unless your employer or a future client is paying.

### 4. CyberArk Partner Ecosystem (For First Client)

CyberArk maintains an authorized partner network of system integrators who implement CyberArk at enterprise clients. Large firms in this network include Deloitte, Accenture, and Optiv. There are also many mid-size regional partners. These firms hire PAM consultants — both as employees and as subcontractors.

**The employment fork — Month 8 decision point:**

After earning your Guardian cert in M8, you will have 3 CyberArk certs and demonstrable hands-on lab experience. At this point you have a real decision to make:

- Option A: Continue the roadmap as a sole proprietor. Reach M18, then launch your consulting practice independently.
- Option B: Apply for a junior or mid-level PAM consultant role at a CyberArk partner firm. Get paid while finishing Phase 3. Build real client references and case studies. Launch your own practice from a position of strength at M24 instead of M18.

Neither path is wrong. Option B is lower risk for the first client acquisition problem — you build references while still employed. Option A gives more control over your schedule and more focused time for OSCP preparation.

If you choose Option B: search for CyberArk partner firms in your region. Look for job titles such as "CyberArk implementation consultant" or "PAM consultant." Your Guardian cert plus portfolio lab projects qualify you for junior roles at most of these firms. The certification is a minimum filter they use — the lab projects differentiate you from other candidates who only studied.

## First Client Acquisition

The hardest part of consulting is the first paid engagement. Do not wait until M18 to think about this. The strategies below are ordered roughly from easiest to hardest to close.

**1. Former employer as first client:**

Your current employer already knows your skills and can evaluate them without relying on third-party signals like certifications. A PAM health check or Conjur architecture review for your current or former company is the most natural first engagement. The trust barrier is already resolved. It is also the easiest reference to obtain — you know the contact and they know your work.

**2. CyberArk partner subcontracting:**

Larger CyberArk partners regularly subcontract overflow implementation work to independent consultants. Contact 5-10 mid-size CyberArk partners in M14-M16. Introduce yourself — your certifications, your OSCP track, and the fact that you are available for subcontracting. This will typically come in at lower rates ($75-125/hr) but provides real client project experience and references, which are worth more than the hourly delta at this stage.

**3. LinkedIn inbound from articles:**

If your Phase 3 articles are specific and technically honest, people will message you. An article on deploying Conjur in Kubernetes will attract DevOps and platform engineering teams who want to do the same thing and cannot find someone who has done it. Respond to every comment and every message, even brief ones. The reader who leaves a comment is warmer than any cold outreach you will ever do.

**4. Bug bounty and HackTheBox writeup referrals:**

Active HackTheBox participation and public machine writeups generate credibility in the offensive security community. Occasionally, companies that read your writeups or see your profile will reach out about penetration testing work. This is a slow channel and unpredictable, but it is real and costs nothing other than time you are already spending.

## HackTheBox Profile as a Portfolio Artifact

HackTheBox maintains a public profile showing your ranking, completed machines, and challenge completions. This profile is visible to hiring managers and prospective clients in offensive security and is treated as a credible signal of practical ability — more so than most certifications below OSCP level.

- Target rank: Pro Hacker (top 25%) by M12, Elite Hacker or better by M17.
- Include your HackTheBox profile URL on your LinkedIn profile and your consulting resume, listed alongside your OSCP certification once earned.
- Write 1-2 machine writeups on a personal blog after those machines retire from the active pool. These are read by other security professionals and build your name recognition over time. They also demonstrate written communication ability, which matters for consulting.
